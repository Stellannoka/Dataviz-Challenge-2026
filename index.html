The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific Islands, and how the gap between climate risk and preparedness shapes those costs.

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

Climate change is making hazards such as floods, droughts, tropical cyclones and sea-level rise more frequent, and in many cases more intense. Yet the damage they cause depends not only on the hazards themselves, but also on how prepared countries are to withstand and recover from them.

For many Pacific Island Countries, that preparedness has not kept pace. For more than two decades, many have ranked among the world's most climate-vulnerable while remaining among the least ready to adapt. That gap between risk and readiness has barely shifted.

Most at Risk, Least Prepared

Over the years, many Pacific Island Countries have ranked among the world's most climate-vulnerable and least ready to adapt.

Set against every country in the world, many Pacific Island Countries occupy a distinctive position: among the most climate-vulnerable, yet comparatively less ready to adapt. While individual countries shift over time, this broader pattern changes remarkably little.

What that pattern means becomes clear when climate-related disasters strike. The impacts are first seen in the people affected, and in the livelihoods they depend on.

For several Pacific Island Countries, the number of livelihoods disrupted was nearly as high as the number of people affected by disasters.

Behind those figures are households whose farms, fisheries, businesses and other sources of income were disrupted, leaving recovery to depend not only on repairing damaged infrastructure, but also on restoring the means to earn a living.

Hazards can't be prevented. Their impacts can be reduced.

Pacific Island Countries cannot choose the climate hazards they face. Vanuatu cannot move beyond the cyclone belt, nor can Tuvalu raise its islands above rising seas. What can change is their readiness to withstand and recover from those hazards.

Strengthening infrastructure, expanding early warning systems and building resilient institutions can reduce the human and economic costs of climate-related disasters.

Yet the scale is beyond what these economies can carry alone. Across the region, estimated adaptation needs average about a fifth of GDP each year, and for the atoll nations closer to a third. No economy can self-finance costs at that scale, so closing the gap between climate risk and readiness depends on international climate finance.

Readiness Remains Underfunded

The finance needed to close the gap is not arriving at the scale required.

Readiness cannot improve without investment and if adaptation finance flows to the Pacific Islands countries continues at recent levels, it would meet only about one-quarter (26%) of the projected annual adaptation needs for strengthening resilience and preparing for a changing climate.

This helps explain why the gap between vulnerability and readiness has proved so persistent. As climate risks continue to grow faster than investment, many Pacific Island Countries remain among the world's most vulnerable while still lacking the readiness needed to reduce future impacts.

Conclusion

For two decades, the gap between climate vulnerability and readiness has remained remarkably persistent across the Pacific Island Countries. And behind that gap are people whose homes, livelihoods and communities continue to bear the consequences of climate-related disasters.

Climate hazards cannot always be prevented. But the severity of their impacts depends, in part, on how prepared countries are before they arrive. Closing the gap between vulnerability and readiness means investing in resilience so that communities can better withstand the hazards they cannot avoid.

Methodology

This project was created as an entry for the Pacific DataViz Challenge 2026. It was built with React, D3.js, and styled with Tailwind CSS.

It brings together multiple datasets to examine climate vulnerability, disaster impacts and resilience across Pacific Island Countries. Where appropriate, indicators are presented both as absolute values and population-adjusted measures to support meaningful comparisons between countries of different sizes.

The datasets used throughout the project are cited and linked in the caption of each visualisation.
