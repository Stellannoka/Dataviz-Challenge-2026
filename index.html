The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific, and the gap between the harm it faces and its capacity to withstand it.

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

Human-induced climate change is taking a growing toll on communities, infrastructure, and natural ecosystems in the Pacific Island region.

Across the the region, a single climate-related disaster can directly affect a large share of a nation's people and erase a significant part of its economy in a single year — and the smallest states bear the heaviest blows relative to their size. Yet beneath this damage lies a widening gap: between the harm a nation is exposed to, and its capacity to turn resources into protection against it. As climate pressures intensify, that gap carries a rising cost — measured not only in economic loss, but in livelihoods disrupted and communities left exposed.

These were not just a few bad years. They are part of a broader pattern of climate-related disasters whose costs are increasingly visible across the Pacific. The losses are already mounting. Whether the region can keep pace with the risks ahead is another matter.

Most at Risk, Least Prepared

The University of Notre Dame's ND-GAIN Index measures two sides of climate resilience. Vulnerability is how much harm climate change is likely to do to a country's food, water, health, land and infrastructure. Readiness is how well a country can attract investment and turn it into protection through its economy, government and institutions.

Set against every country in the world, the Pacific clusters in the chart's most precarious corner: highly vulnerable and poorly prepared. The Solomon Islands and the Federated States of Micronesia rank among the most vulnerable countries anywhere. Papua New Guinea is less prepared than four out of five nations.

And Vanuatu, the Marshall Islands and Tonga — among the countries hardest hit by disasters in the years just shown — sit there too. And they have for a long time. For two decades, the region's position has barely shifted. The countries facing the greatest climate risks remain among the least ready to withstand them.

Risk is fixed. Readiness is built.

A country cannot choose how much climate change threatens it. Vanuatu cannot move out of the cyclone belt; Tuvalu cannot raise its islands above the sea. That half of the gap; the risk: is largely fixed but the other half is not.

Readiness is built: with sea walls and early-warning systems, with stronger buildings and trained responders, with the institutions that turn plans into protection. And unlike geography, these things can be changed.

If risk is the problem countries inherit, readiness is the problem they can solve. And solving it costs money. This is what turns a climate problem into a financing one. Building readiness requires resources many Pacific nations cannot afford on their own. Yet the money exists. In fact, much of it has already been pledged. The question is whether it is reaching the places that need it most.

The Money to Prepare Is Promised, Not Delivered

The shortfall is stark. The Pacific needs about $3.3 billion a year to build that protection. Only about a quarter of it is ever delivered — a gap of nearly $2.4 billion, every year. The resources to close the distance between risk and readiness exist on paper. They do not arrive.

This is a failure of plumbing, not just pledges. Multilateral funds — the primary channel for the most vulnerable nations — disburse more slowly than bilateral aid, stalled by complex approval processes and the limited administrative capacity of small island states. The resilience gap is, at its core, a financing gap: the means to close it exist on paper, but rarely in the places they are needed.

The losses recorded here are not the work of the weather alone. They are the cost of a gap — between the harm these nations face and their capacity to turn resources into protection — and that gap is built, and maintained.

It is maintained by a vulnerability that has not eased. It is maintained by a readiness that cannot climb faster than the investment available to lift it. And it is maintained, at its root, by money promised in full and delivered in fraction — a shortfall measured not in pledges, but in the homes, harvests and livelihoods left unprotected when the next storm arrives.

The cost of the gap is already being paid — year after year, by the people in the places least equipped to bear it. What remains unpaid is the far smaller sum it would take to begin closing it. Until that arrives, the gap will keep its price. And the Pacific will keep paying it.
