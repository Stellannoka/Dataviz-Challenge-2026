The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific Islands, and the gap between the harm it faces and its capacity to withstand it.

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

Human-induced climate change is taking a growing toll on communities, infrastructure, and natural ecosystems in the Pacific Islands.

Across the the region, a single climate-related disaster can directly affect a large share of a nation's people and erase a significant part of its economy in a single year and the smallest states bear the heaviest blows relative to their size. Yet beneath this damage lies a widening gap: between the harm a nation is exposed to, and its capacity to turn resources into protection against it. As climate pressures intensify, that gap carries a rising cost — measured not only in economic loss, but in livelihoods disrupted and communities left exposed.

Readiness on the other hand, measures a region's ability to leverage investments and turn them into action based on economic, governance, and social factors.

Set against every country in the world, Pacific nations rank among the most vulnerable to climate change. The Solomon Islands and the Federated States of Micronesia rank third and fourth globally for climate vulnerability, while Papua New Guinea is less prepared than four out of five countries. Together with Vanuatu, the Marshall Islands and Tonga, they cluster in one of the chart's most precarious quadrants: highly vulnerable and among the least ready to adapt.

The picture has changed little over time. Over the past two decades, Pacific nations have remained consistently more vulnerable than the global average while lagging in readiness. The countries facing some of the world's greatest climate risks are still among those least prepared to withstand them.

Risk is fixed. Readiness is built.

A country cannot choose how much climate change threatens it. Vanuatu cannot move out of the cyclone belt; Tuvalu cannot raise its islands above the sea. That half of the gap; the risk: is largely fixed.

Readiness is built: with sea walls and early-warning systems, with stronger buildings and trained responders, with the institutions that turn plans into protection. Unlike geography, these are investments that governments can make.

That distinction matters. If risk is the challenge countries inherit, readiness is the challenge they can improve. But improving it is inevitably going to cost money. For many Pacific nations, the countries most exposed to climate impacts are also among those least able to pay for the protection they need. Public and grant-based climate finance is therefore essential to closing that divide.

The Pacific Region Receives a Fraction of What It Needs

The Pacific region is estimated to need about US$3.3 billion each year to adapt to future climate change. But the money falls far short. Only about US$900 million in climate finance reaches the region each year, leaving an estimated annual shortfall of US$2.4 billion.

For small island states, the funding shortfall limits how quickly countries can build the protections needed to reduce climate risk. As a result, the gap between vulnerability and readiness persists.

The Pacific's smallest nations are among the world's most exposed to climate change, yet they remain among the least prepared to withstand it. The funding needed to build that resilience continues to fall well short of estimated needs.

Climate change will continue to test the region. Whether future disasters become catastrophes will depend not only on the risks the Pacific faces, but on its ability to close the gap between vulnerability and readiness.
