The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific and why the gap behind them stays open

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

In the Pacific region, human-induced climate change is intensifying the disasters that strike island communities, posing a growing threat not only to livelihoods and the health of the region's ecosystems, but to the well-being of the people who depend on them.

For the small island developing states, this burden is not shared evenly. When disaster strikes, the smallest nations bear the heaviest blows relative to their size — a single event can directly affect a vast share of their people and erase a significant part of their economy in one year. Behind that impact lies a second, slower-moving divide — a gap between the harm climate change is likely to inflict on each nation, and its capacity to convert resources into protection against it.

The gap carries a cost, and it is already being paid. This essay traces what it costs, why it exists, and why it has yet to be closed.

The Costs Are Measured in Lives and Livelihoods

The lives upended are only half the story. The same storms strike economies far too small to absorb them — and the bill lands in a single year.

A Persistent Gap Between Risk and Readiness

Vulnerability measures how much harm climate change is likely to do to a nation’s essential systems — its food, water, health, habitat and infrastructure — accounting not only for exposure, but for how sensitive those systems are and how readily they adapt. Readiness measures something different: a nation’s capacity to attract investment and turn it into protection, through its economy, its governance and its institutions. The distance between the two — high vulnerability met with low readiness — is the gap this report is named for: great harm to guard against, and little capacity to mobilise the resources to do it.

Papua New Guinea sits furthest into that corner, with the Federated States of Micronesia and Vanuatu close behind — the nations facing the most, and equipped with the least. More broadly, most Pacific nations lie above the global median for vulnerability and below it for readiness: more exposed to harm than the typical country, and less able to convert resources into protection.

The gap has held for two decades. Readiness has risen, but largely in step with global progress, while vulnerability has not fallen to meet it. The question is what keeps it open.

The Money to Prepare Is Promised, Not Delivered

The shortfall is stark. The Pacific needs about $3.3 billion a year to build that protection. Only about a quarter of it is ever delivered — a gap of nearly $2.4 billion, every year. The resources to close the distance between risk and readiness exist on paper. They do not arrive.

This is a failure of plumbing, not just pledges. Multilateral funds — the primary channel for the most vulnerable nations — disburse more slowly than bilateral aid, stalled by complex approval processes and the limited administrative capacity of small island states. The resilience gap is, at its core, a financing gap: the means to close it exist on paper, but rarely in the places they are needed.

The losses recorded here are not the work of the weather alone. They are the cost of a gap — between the harm these nations face and their capacity to turn resources into protection — and that gap is built, and maintained.

It is maintained by a vulnerability that has not eased. It is maintained by a readiness that cannot climb faster than the investment available to lift it. And it is maintained, at its root, by money promised in full and delivered in fraction — a shortfall measured not in pledges, but in the homes, harvests and livelihoods left unprotected when the next storm arrives.

The cost of the gap is already being paid — year after year, by the people in the places least equipped to bear it. What remains unpaid is the far smaller sum it would take to begin closing it. Until that arrives, the gap will keep its price. And the Pacific will keep paying it.
