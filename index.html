The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific Islands, and the gap between the harm they face and their readiness to meet it.

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

Human-caused climate change is placing growing pressure on the Pacific Islands and the communities that call them home.

Across the region, a single climate-related disaster can directly affect a large share of a nation's population in a single year. Yet beneath these losses lies another challenge: the gap between countries' vulnerability to climate change and their readiness to adapt. As climate pressures intensify, that gap carries a growing cost—measured not only in disrupted livelihoods, but also in damaged infrastructure and communities left exposed.

The Cost of Disasters

Climate-related events directly affected hundreds of thousands of people across the Pacific Islands in 2020

Disasters do more than affect people directly. They also disrupt the livelihoods that people depend on.

For several Pacific Island nations, the number of livelihoods disrupted was nearly as high as the number of people affected by disasters. The figures show how quickly disaster impacts can extend beyond immediate exposure to affect people's ability to earn a living.

What they do not explain is why some countries experience far greater impacts than others. That requires looking beyond disasters themselves to the conditions that shape vulnerability and resilience.

Most at Risk, Least Prepared

For more than two decades, Pacific nations have remained among the world's most vulnerable and least prepared.

Set against every country in the world, many Pacific Island nations face a double challenge: high vulnerability to climate change and limited readiness to adapt. Together, these conditions leave countries more exposed to the socio-economic impacts of climate-related disasters.

More concerning is how little this relationship has changed. For more than two decades, Pacific Island nations have remained concentrated where high vulnerability meets comparatively low readiness. The gap between climate risk and preparedness has remained largely unchanged.

Risk is fixed. Readiness is built.

Pacific Island nations cannot choose the climate hazards they face. Vanuatu cannot move beyond the cyclone belt, nor can Tuvalu raise its islands above rising seas.

Readiness, however, can be strengthened through investments in stronger infrastructure, early warning systems and resilient institutions.

Many Pacific Island nations cannot finance these investments on their own. Building the readiness needed to reduce the impacts of climate change therefore depends heavily on international climate finance.

Readiness Remains Underfunded

Climate change finance flows to the Pacific Islands fall far short of estimated needs

Despite their high exposure to climate risks, Pacific Island nations receive only a fraction of the finance needed to strengthen their resilience. Current funding falls well short of estimated adaptation needs.

The consequence extends beyond delayed projects. When investment cannot keep pace with rising climate risks, readiness improves more slowly than vulnerability changes. That helps explain why many Pacific Island nations have remained among the world's most vulnerable and least prepared for more than two decades.

Conclusion

The Pacific's smallest nations are already living with the consequences of the gap between climate vulnerability and readiness. The costs are measured in people directly affected, livelihoods disrupted and the limited capacity to prepare for future climate impacts.

Climate change will continue to test the region. Whether future disasters become temporary shocks or lasting setbacks will depend on how quickly that gap can be narrowed.

Methodology

This project was built with React, D3.js, and Tailwind CSS as an interactive scrollytelling experience.

It brings together multiple datasets to examine disaster impacts and resilience across Pacific Island countries. Where appropriate, indicators are presented both as absolute values and population-adjusted measures to support meaningful comparisons between countries of different sizes.

The datasets used throughout the project are cited and linked in the caption of each visualisation.
