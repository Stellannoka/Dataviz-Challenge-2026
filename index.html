Climate Change

The Cost of the Gap

What climate disasters cost the Pacific Islands, and how the gap between vulnerability and readiness shapes those costs.

By Stellamaris Nnoka

August 25, 2026

Climate change is making hazards such as floods, droughts, tropical cyclones and sea-level rise more frequent, and in many cases more intense. Yet the damage they cause depends not only on the hazards themselves, but also on how prepared countries are to withstand and recover from them.

For many Pacific Island Countries, that preparedness has not kept pace. For more than two decades, every one of the twelve countries in the data has remained in the world's high-vulnerability half. Readiness has varied, with some countries improving more than others. Yet none has escaped the region's persistently high climate vulnerability. Together, those patterns have changed remarkably little over the past years.

High Vulnerability, Uneven Readiness

For two decades, every Pacific Island Country has remained in the world's high-vulnerability half but readiness has varied far more.

In the ND-GAIN framework, higher readiness means countries are better positioned to translate investment into adaptation, but their adaptation needs remain high. Even where readiness has improved, it has not kept pace with the region's persistently high vulnerability, leaving a gap between the need to adapt and the capacity to do so.

Although individual countries shift over time, this overall pattern remains remarkably consistent. Its consequences become most apparent when climate-related disasters strike. with the impacts first felt by affected populations and the livelihoods on which they depend.

The Marshall Islands recorded the starkest example, where livelihoods disrupted were equivalent to more than nine in ten people affected.

Behind those figures are households whose farms, fisheries and businesses were disrupted, leaving recovery to depend not only on repairing damaged infrastructure, but also on restoring the means to earn a living. These impacts reflect not only the hazards countries face, but also how prepared they are to withstand and recover from them.

Hazards cannot be chosen. Their impacts can be reduced.

Pacific Island Countries cannot choose the climate hazards they face. Vanuatu cannot move beyond the cyclone belt, nor can Tuvalu raise its islands above rising seas. Nor can these countries choose their small populations or geographic isolation, both of which leave them with fewer resources to absorb and recover from climate shocks. What can change is their readiness to adapt to and recover from those hazards.

Turning readiness into effective adaptation requires sustained investment in infrastructure, early warning systems and resilient institutions. Yet many Pacific Island Countries cannot finance those investments from domestic resources alone.

Across the region, estimated adaptation needs average just over one-fifth of GDP each year, and exceed one-third for some atoll nations. Meeting needs on that scale therefore depends heavily on international climate finance.

Adaptation Remains Underfunded

Despite its importance, the adaptation finance reaching Pacific Island Countries falls well short of meeting estimated adaptation needs. At recent funding levels, it would meet only about one-quarter (26%) of projected annual adaptation finance needs. That regional figure is an average. The share of each country's estimated needs met by recent finance varies widely.

Without investment that better matches the scale of adaptation needs, reducing vulnerability and strengthening resilience will remain difficult. However high a country's readiness becomes, it can only translate the finance that arrives into adaptation.

That is the gap, and its cost is measured not in dollars alone, but in the people, livelihoods and communities left most exposed.
